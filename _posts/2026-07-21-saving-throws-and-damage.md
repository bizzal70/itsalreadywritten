D&D 5e keeps saving throws and damage plain. An effect names its trigger, a target rolls the save, and you apply the result a single time. A failed save means the full stated consequence. A successful save means whatever the effect says a success grants, usually half damage or none. There is no second bite, no re-roll for spite.

The confusion arrives with groups. When a single effect forces two or more targets to save against it at the same moment, such as Fireball, you roll the damage once. That one damage number applies to everyone caught in the area. Each creature makes its own save against that shared total. You do not roll separate damage for each target, and one creature's success does not change another's result. Same number, individual saves, individual outcomes.

That distinction settles most arguments before they start. The dragon's breath does not weaken because one rogue dodged, and the wizard does not get to reroll the die for the second goblin.

**At the table:** Cast Fireball at three bandits, roll 8d6 once for 28 fire damage, then have each bandit make its own Dexterity save, applying 28 on a failure and 14 on a success.
