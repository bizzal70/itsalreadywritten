Most players read a spell and immediately reach for the damage number. That is the wrong first question. A spell is a tool with a specific shape, a specific trigger, and a specific failure mode, and the players who get the most out of the book are the ones who treat "when do I cast this" as the primary decision. Spell Use Case is the discipline of matching the tool to the moment. The rules already tell you how; the manual assumes you read past line one.

Start with the joke spell. Word of Radiance is a cantrip that deals 1d6 radiant damage and forces a Constitution saving throw on every creature you choose within a 5-foot sphere centered on you. That last part is the whole point. Cast it at a single high-Constitution enemy and you will roll dice, watch them save, and feel foolish. Cast it when three or four low-Constitution creatures have clustered against you, and it becomes efficient area damage that no other cantrip in your list can replicate. The spell did not change. The situation did. Word of Radiance is a crowd-control cantrip disguised as a throwaway.

Acid Splash teaches the same lesson from a different angle. It creates an acidic bubble that detonates in a 5-foot radius, and any creature caught in the blast makes a Dexterity saving throw or takes 1d6 Acid damage. Read that as "small damage" and you will waste it on the wrong targets, specifically the ones built to shrug off saves. Read it as a positioning tool and you notice it can be used for access and information, not just another reroll against a boss. Ask what the acid opens, spooks, or forces to move before you ask what it hurts.

Aid is where the "spell is a verb" idea stops being about damage entirely. Aid grants a flat increase of 5 hit points, both current and maximum, to as many as three targets for its duration. Players file it under healing and move on, which misses the fact that those hit points sit on your allies for hours. That duration is leverage. It sets up a later fight, buffers a scout walking into a dangerous area, or quietly changes the math of a negotiation where everyone knows your party can take a hit. Aid is a setup spell that happens to look like a heal.

Alarm rewards the same forward thinking, and punishes players who ignore its shape. It is a 1st-level abjuration spell with a 30-foot range, an 8-hour duration, and a 20-foot cube area. Cast it carelessly and the cube covers empty floor. Cast it at the actual chokepoint, the doorway or the one corridor everyone must use, and you convert eight hours of watch into reliable information about intrusion. Alarm is only as good as the square footage you point it at.

Alter Self closes the loop by reminding you that a spell's cost is part of its use case. It changes your physical form, but it requires concentration, which means damage and environmental strain can end it at the worst possible moment. Players who wear it into a brawl learn this the hard way. The spell earns its keep in reconnaissance and infiltration, where nobody is rolling to break your concentration and the altered appearance actually does the job. The mistake is not the spell. It is the setting.

Five spells, one habit. Word of Radiance, Acid Splash, Aid, Alarm, and Alter Self all punish the "what's the damage" reflex and reward the player who reads the trigger, the area, the duration, and the cost first.

**Running it:** Before you spend a slot, say out loud what problem this spell solves right now. If you cannot name the problem, you have picked the wrong spell.
